About The Tour


Pujo in Kolkata is like a carnival that takes over the city! Imagine vibrant streets decked out in dazzling lights and ornate pandals that pop up like magical kingdoms overnight. Everywhere you turn, there's the tantalizing aroma of street food—think crispy kathi rolls and melt-in-your-mouth mishti! People from all walks of life come together, dressed in their festive best, ready to dance to the beats of dhak and sway with the rhythm of dhaaki naach. It's a whirlwind of color, culture, and camaraderie, where traditions blend seamlessly with modern-day excitement. Pujo in Kolkata isn't just a festival; it's a lively, unforgettable celebration that captures the heart and soul of the City of Joy!.

Trip highlights



Pandal Hopping

Pandal hopping during Pujo is an exhilarating cultural experience where elaborate, artistically themed structures house idols of Goddess Durga. The city comes alive with lights, music, and vibrant decorations, as locals and tourists alike visit various pandals to admire their unique designs. Each pandal showcases creative expressions, often reflecting social themes or traditional art forms. This festive tradition captures the spirit of community and celebration, making it a highlight of Kolkata's cultural calendar.

Bonedi Bari

Bonedi Bari Durga Puja offers a glimpse into the city's aristocratic past, as age-old, opulent family homes (bonedi baris) celebrate the festival with traditional grandeur. These pujas are marked by elaborate rituals, age-old customs, and the unique charm of heritage architecture. Unlike the more commercial pandals, Bonedi Bari pujas retain a sense of intimacy and cultural authenticity. Visiting these ancestral homes during Durga Puja provides a rich cultural experience, steeped in history and tradition.

Kolabau Snan

Kolabau Snan, a revered ritual during Durga Puja, involves the ceremonial bathing of the Nabapatrika, also known as Kolabau or the banana bride. This ritual symbolizes the invocation of Goddess Durga's presence in the plantain tree, which is adorned with a sari and vermilion. The Kolabau is taken to the river at dawn for a ritual bath, signifying purification and the beginning of the puja festivities. This age-old tradition highlights the deep connection between nature and spirituality in Bengali culture.

Dhuno Purono

Dhuno Purano is a traditional ritual performed during Pujo, where married women offer prayers while holding burning incense (dhuno) in their hands. They perform this ritual in front of the Durga idol, often balancing the burning dhuno on a brass plate on their heads and hands. This act is believed to purify the surroundings and invoke the goddess's blessings. It is a visually striking and spiritually significant part of the Pujo celebrations, adding to the festival's vibrant atmosphere.

Bhog

Bhog during Pujo is a sacred offering of food to the goddess, later distributed among devotees as prasad. This ritual involves preparing a variety of vegetarian dishes, including khichuri, labra (mixed vegetable curry), and sweets, with utmost devotion and purity. The bhog is first offered to Goddess Durga and then served to devotees, symbolizing communal harmony and divine blessings. Partaking in the bhog is considered highly auspicious, and it enhances the spiritual and festive experience of Durga Puja.

Sandhi Pujo

Sandhi Puja, a pivotal ritual during Pujo, marks the transition between Ashtami and Navami, the eighth and ninth days of the festival. This sacred ceremony is performed during the precise moment when Goddess Durga is believed to have vanquished the demon Mahishasura, symbolizing the triumph of good over evil. The ritual involves lighting 108 lamps, offering 108 lotus flowers, and performing arati amidst the resonant sounds of dhak (traditional drums) and chants.

Dhunuchi Dance

Dhunuchi dance during Pujo is a mesmerizing ritual where devotees perform a traditional dance holding a smoking dhunuchi (earthen incense burner). This dance, accompanied by rhythmic beats of dhak drums, invokes a sense of fervor and devotion. The aromatic smoke from the dhunuchi is believed to purify the air and please the goddess. Dhunuchi dance adds a surreal and sensory dimension to the already electric festive atmosphere.


Sindoor Khela

Sindoor Khela is a vibrant and joyous ritual performed on the last day of Pujo, known as Vijayadashami. Married women apply sindoor (vermilion powder) to the goddess's forehead and feet, and then smear it on each other, symbolizing the wish for marital bliss and longevity. This ritual marks the farewell to Goddess Durga as she returns to her celestial abode. Sindoor Khela, with its lively and colorful display, embodies the essence of unity, festivity, and the celebration of womanhood.

Durga Visarjan

Durga Visarjan, the immersion of Goddess Durga's idol, is a poignant and grand finale to Pujo. Held on Vijayadashami, the tenth day, devotees bid an emotional farewell to the goddess with processions filled with music, dance, and chants. The idols are carried to the river, symbolizing Durga's return to her divine realm, while prayers are offered for her swift return next year. This ritual marks the heartfelt emotions of parting, leaving an indelible mark on the hearts of the devotees.

Authentic Bengali Food

Kolkata made it to the “World’s Top 10 Food Destinations” in 2023 and while we are in the city, we won’t like to miss on any opportunity to feast on authentic Bengali food.! Food in Kolkata is a delightful blend of flavors and traditions, showcasing the region's rich culinary heritage. Signature dishes include aromatic fish curries like Macher Jhol, the iconic Shorshe Ilish (hilsa fish in mustard sauce), and hearty vegetarian preparations such as Shukto. Sweet delicacies like Rasgulla and Sandesh add a perfect end to the meal.

Colonial Calcutta

Kolkata, once the capital of British India, is steeped in history and architectural grandeur. The city boasts iconic colonial-era structures like the Victoria Memorial, Howrah Bridge, and St. Paul's Cathedral, reflecting its imperial past. During the British Raj, Kolkata was a hub of trade, education, and culture, shaping much of its modern identity. While we will be very busy and awe-struck by the grandeur of Pujo celebrations, we will take out time to stroll around the Colonial monuments of the City of Joy!

Location details


Kolkata, the capital city of West Bengal, India, is situated along the western bank of the Hooghly River, a distributary of the Ganges, which plays a significant role in the city's geography and economy.

Kolkata experiences a tropical wet and dry climate, also known as a tropical savanna climate.
In October, Kolkata experiences a transition period from the rainy monsoon season to the cooler winter months. Daytime temperatures typically range from around 25°C to 32°C (77°F to 90°F). Humidity levels begin to decrease as the monsoon withdraws, leading to more pleasant and drier conditions throughout the month. The weather in October is generally characterized by sunny days with occasional cloudy periods. Towards the end of the month, temperatures start to drop further as Kolkata transitions into the winter season.
