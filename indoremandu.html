About The Tour


The months of 'Aashadh' and 'Saawan' bring a whole new season of celebrations in Indian culture. There is no parallel in the world to the celebration of rains as in India - festivals, feasts, songs, and needless to say that it has inspired countless poets and writers, be it the rains and clouds forming the backdrop to Kalidas's Meghdoot as early as 5th CE, or music set in backdrop of rains in Indian cinema.

Team TheBrownTicket invites you to a journey to "Mandu" during monsoons, a time when the natural beauty mixed with the historical ruins of Mandu, makes it a divine experience!
And Mandu also stands as witness to the legendary romance of Rani Roopmati and Baz Bahadur... The tales of their love embedded in the monuments, the folklore, and well even in names of hotels and the menus of local restaurants!!

We will also eat our way through the street foods @ Saraffa Bazaar, as we explore the historical city of the Maratha Holkars, "Indore" and eventually end our journey at Maheshwar, where we gaze at the swollen Narmada as we explore this city of temples and ghats and understand the world-famous Maheswari silk weaves!

Trip highlights



Rajwada, Indore

Rajwada is a grand residential palace complex built in the mid-eighteenth century by the Holkars of the Maratha empire.The seven-storeyed building blends Maratha, Mughal, and French architectural styles and is brimming with carved stones, wooden jaalis, jharokhas, and chhatris. The Palace also serves as a museum that displays the Holkars’ legacy and possessions of historical relevance.

Lalbagh palace, Indore

Lal Bagh Palace is class and grandeur personified. Designed by Bernard Triggs, Lal Bagh Palace is a blend of renaissance, paladin and baroque elements in architecture, and rococo and neo-classical in furniture. Adding more beauty to the lavishness of the palace, there are Belgium stained glass windows, grand chandeliers, rich Persian carpets and Greek mythological reliefs.

Sarafa bazar street food

Sarafa bazar is a vibrant night time street food market in Indore. What is a "Sarafa bazar" (jewellery market) in the daytime, turns into a bustling food joint during the night, offering the street foods ranging from the essential Poha Jalebi to the very local, Bhutte ka khees; the sweetness of lassi and rabris; and top it up with a ‘paan’. The lively atmosphere is beyond words and can only be experienced.

Krishnapura Chhatri, Indore

Also known as Holkar Chhatris, the complex is a collection of three chhatris or Cenotaphs, built in the memory of royal Holkar family members. Located in a quaint corner of the city at the bank of river Kahn, these cenotaphs are popular for their elegant Maratha architecture and sculptures. Situated around 500m from Rajwada, this is just the perfect place to relax and end a tiring day.

Jahaz Mahal, Mandu

Jahaz Mahal, or the 'Ship Palace' in Mandu, built in the 15th century, is a two storeyed structure that represents the classical phase of architecture in Mandu. The structure stands on a strip of land, sandwiched between Munj Talab (Lake) and Kapur Talab (Lake), which imparts it the appearance of a ship floating in water. And the monsoon clouds and rains just makes the feeling of "ship ride" complete.

Hindola Mahal, Mandu

Hindola Mahal, or the “Swinging Palace” in Mandu, built in the 15th century is a large Durbaar or meeting hall. The whole structure gives the impression of being on a swing because of the slanting walls and arches. The outer walls or the buttresses are inclined at an impressive 77 degrees, giving the palace its name. And to see the grandeur of the palace on celluloid, pull out the famous Lata tai song, “naam gum jayega”!

Hoshang Shah Tomb, Mandu

Built in the 15th century, Hoshang Shah Tomb is one of India's earliest marble monuments, showcasing exquisite Afghan architecture. The tomb is renowned for its stunning marble latticework, intricate carvings, and elegant domes, which exemplify the refined craftsmanship of the period. The tomb's serene setting and architectural grandeur attract history enthusiasts and tourists alike.

Jami Masjid, Mandu

The Jami Masjid in Mandu, India, is a grand mosque inspired by the Great Mosque of Damascus, built in the 15th century. This magnificent structure features a vast courtyard, large prayer hall, and intricately designed domes and arches. The use of large domes not only adds to the aesthetic appeal but also serves to amplify sound within the prayer hall, creating an acoustically harmonious environment.

Roopmati Pavilion, Mandu

The Rani Roopmati Pavilion in Mandu is a historically significant structure associated with the legendary romance between Rani Roopmati, a poetess and singer, and Sultan Baz Bahadur of Malwa. Perched on a hilltop, it offers stunning views of the surrounding landscape. The pavilion was built in the 16th century to provide Rani Roopmati with a view of the sacred Narmada River, which she revered deeply.

Baj Bahadur Palace, Mandu

The palace is designed with large courtyards, open terraces, and grand halls that were used for musical performances and court gatherings. Set against the backdrop of lush hills, the palace offers panoramic views of the Rewa Kund, a sacred pond. The acoustics of the palace are meticulously planned to enhance musical performances, a testament to Baz Bahadur's passion for music.

Maheshwar

Maheshwar is a historic town on the banks of the Narmada River. It is renowned for its majestic temples and forts. Once the capital of the Holkar dynasty, it was revived by Rani Ahilyabai Holkar in the 18th century. The town is famous for its intricate Maheshwari sarees, handwoven with unique designs. It’s scenic temples, vibrant ghats and the huge expanse of Narmada makes it a significant spiritual and historical destination.

Location details


Mandu, also known as Mandav, is a historic town located in the Dhar district of Madhya Pradesh, India.

Geographically, it is situated on a plateau in the Vindhya Range, approximately 633 meters (2,077 feet) above sea level. The town is perched on the edge of the Malwa plateau, providing stunning views of the surrounding valleys and plains. The region is characterized by rugged terrain, dense forests, and numerous lakes and streams, contributing to its picturesque landscape.

Monsoon (July to September): The monsoon season brings substantial rainfall, with the region receiving an average annual rainfall of about 1,200 mm (47 inches). The rains transform Mandu into a lush, green landscape, replenishing its water bodies and making it a popular time for tourists seeking scenic beauty.
